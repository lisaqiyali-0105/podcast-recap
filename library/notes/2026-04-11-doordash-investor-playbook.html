01
On Moats

The moat nobody could see from the outside

It's always the data that you can't see that kills you. Because if you can see a truck coming at you, you're just going to dodge and get out the way. But if you can't see it, you're dead.

Tony Xu

The Investor Read

DoorDash's defensibility isn't the consumer app — it's the structured dataset built from years of operating in a chaotic physical world. There are 20 decomposable steps in a single delivery, each with its own failure modes: a homesick driver, a broken gate code, an item moved from aisle 6 to aisle 8 with no documentation. None of this exists in any scrapable dataset. It can only be learned by doing millions of orders.

This is a classically underappreciated moat: operational complexity that looks replicable from the outside but requires years and billions of orders to actually build. Mickey from Wolt had a fresh $1B term sheet in front of him and concluded he couldn't beat Tony — choosing to sell instead. That's what an invisible moat looks like from inside the competitor.

Due Diligence Note

Ask for cohort-level on-time delivery improvement trends over time. That curve is the compounding value of physical world learning made visible — and the best proxy for whether the moat is real.

02
On Timing

The 1,000 days of divergence — the contrarian window

The metrics on the inside — you see everything going the direction that you would hope as an entrepreneur. You see repeatability from city A to city B to city C. You see unit economics improving.

Tony Xu

The Investor Read

From 2016 to 2019, DoorDash operated in a state of maximum narrative-to-fundamentals divergence. External headlines: “can't beat Uber, can't make money, toxic sector.” Internal reality: improving unit economics, expanding city repeatability, organic growth with zero marketing budget. Tony couldn't raise while Uber and Amazon were outspending him.

The investors who passed were optimizing for narrative safety. The few who looked inside the metrics captured the majority of returns before the 2019 turnaround. The 1,000 days weren't a near-death story — they were an extended entry window that most investors let close without acting.

Due Diligence Note

A company that survives 3 years of constrained capital with improving fundamentals has proven its unit economics are real, not manufactured by spend. Ask: what did retention, margin, and organic growth look like during the 2016–2019 period, before the narrative recovered?

03
On Market Sizing

The TAM nobody calculated correctly

There were about a million restaurants in the states, and maybe 20 to 25,000 of them offered deliveries. The real grand question was: what about everyone else?

Tony Xu

The Investor Read

The common investor mistake in 2013 was sizing the food delivery market as the existing delivery market — 25k restaurants, mostly pizza. DoorDash's actual TAM was enabling the other 975,000 restaurants to offer delivery for the first time. That's not a market share story; it's a market creation story.

Investors who saw DoorDash as a GrubHub competitor were sizing a rounding error. The expansion into DashMart, fulfillment for Kroger and CVS, and autonomous delivery is all evidence that the real TAM was always “everything inside a city” — and it's still expanding.

Due Diligence Note

What percentage of current revenue comes from non-delivery services — advertising, data intelligence, fulfillment? What is the trajectory and margin profile of those lines vs. the core delivery take rate? This reveals how far the TAM expansion has actually progressed.

04
On Founder Quality

The CEO who still reads every customer complaint

I still do customer support every day. I see them literally every single day. Those are freebies. How lucky am I to actually have a product in which people care enough to actually let me know something went wrong?

Tony Xu

The Investor Read

A public company CEO doing daily customer support at millions-of-orders scale is an extreme outlier. Tony personally debugs order logs and calls dashers after reading 2,000-word complaint emails. This isn't a ritual — it's how he catches the invisible data that dashboards miss.

Companies led by founders who stay close to raw customer signal make fewer catastrophic product decisions. And when the CEO does it, the entire organization can't hide behind aggregate metrics — the cultural norm cascades downward.

Due Diligence Note

Is this behavior institutionalized or bottlenecked on one person? Ask how customer support insights flow into the product roadmap and experimentation pipeline — and what happens to that signal if Tony steps back.

05
On Compounding

Experimentation culture as a compounding return

If you can get the 5% out of tens of thousands of experiments to work in one year, that has the benefit on all your audience for the next year. And then you just keep going.

Tony Xu

The Investor Read

DoorDash runs hundreds of thousands of experiments per week. The 95% failure rate isn't a problem — it's the price of admission for the 5% that ship and permanently raise the baseline. Next year's experiments start from a higher floor. This is a compounding learning system, not a series of independent bets.

This experimentation culture is extraordinarily hard to replicate quickly. It requires organizational discipline, the right instrumentation, a tolerance for failure, and enough order volume to generate statistically significant results. A new entrant can't just decide to run 100k experiments per year — they have to build the cultural and technical infrastructure first, which takes years.

Due Diligence Note

What is the ratio of experiments run per engineer? What percentage reach statistical significance? These numbers reveal whether the experimentation culture is genuine infrastructure or an aspirational talking point in board decks.

06
On Capital Efficiency

Underfunded forces product discipline — and that's a feature

If a company has no ability to compete with budget, you only have one way to compete: build a product that has better retention, better engagement. You have to.

Tony Xu

The Investor Read

Unable to buy growth for three years while Uber and Amazon outspent them, DoorDash was forced to earn every dollar of retention through product quality alone. This built genuine habits in both the team and the customer base — not habits manufactured by discounts or ads.

Companies that grow on marketing spend often have hollow retention curves the moment spend stops. DoorDash's organic growth during 2016–2019 is evidence that their retention was real. This also explains why Mickey from Wolt chose to sell over taking a fresh $1B — more capital wouldn't have closed the operational learning gap.

Due Diligence Note

Pull retention curves stratified by acquisition channel — organic vs. paid. Organic cohorts should show structurally superior retention. If they don't, the product quality signal doesn't exist independently of the marketing budget.

07
On Revenue Expansion

The intelligence platform hiding inside a delivery company

We're giving data back to them: when you're out of stock, when you're underpriced, when there's an opportunity to bundle SKUs that would grow your actual business. Why can't we do those things for you?

Tony Xu

The Investor Read

Every delivery generates data of extraordinary commercial value: real-time demand signals, pricing benchmarks across thousands of merchants, inventory accuracy, customer preference clustering. Merchants paying for intelligence that makes them more profitable is a fundamentally different revenue model than a delivery commission — higher margin, stickier, compounding.

The delivery business is a trojan horse for becoming the operating system of local commerce. The 2014 farmer who called about logistics was asking DoorDash to be his business partner. Tony said “not yet.” DashMart, Kroger fulfillment, autonomous delivery — those are the “yes” that took a decade to build toward.

Due Diligence Note

What is the revenue per merchant relationship beyond delivery commissions? What is the upsell attach rate for advertising, intelligence, and fulfillment — and what does the curve look like over the first 24 months of a merchant relationship?

08
On Autonomous Infrastructure

The “last 10 feet” as an investable infrastructure thesis

It's very hard to drive a ropotaxi into a crowded hub of merchants and actually somehow find parking and get access to the products by itself. We built DoorDash Dot, which can travel on the road but also on the sidewalk and in bike lanes — a much smaller form factor that solves the last 10 feet problem.

Tony Xu

The Investor Read

Competitors in autonomous mobility are optimizing for the highway problem. DoorDash is optimizing for the access problem — the final 10 feet of every delivery. The ability to navigate sidewalks, bike lanes, mall interiors, and crowded merchant hubs is an operational advantage that general-purpose robotaxis structurally cannot replicate.

This is not a product feature — it's an infrastructure thesis. Purpose-built urban delivery vehicles, deployed at scale across DoorDash's existing merchant network, position the company as the physical layer of city-level commerce. Six years from first internal project to road deployment in Arizona. That timeline is the moat: it cannot be shortcut by capital alone.

Due Diligence Note

What is the staged CapEx roadmap for DoorDash Dot from Arizona pilot to national deployment? At what unit density does autonomous last-mile delivery become margin-accretive vs. human dasher delivery — and how far is the current deployment from that threshold?

09
On Network Effects

Community identity as a network effect — the softer moat

The personality is given by who the businesses are. And therefore, you and your friends want to go frequent and hang out in those places. That's what makes a city feel awesome. And so I think that's an eternal mission worth fighting for.

Tony Xu

The Investor Read

Tony elevated DoorDash from a service provider to an economic utility — a guardian of local community identity. This emotional and civic mission creates a retention mechanism for merchants and consumers that pricing models alone can't quantify. Local economies instinctively favor platforms that visibly support local businesses.

This is the moat that doesn't show up in a spreadsheet. A platform embedded in the social fabric of neighborhoods is far more durable than one competing purely on take rates. It's also structurally difficult for a national incumbent like Amazon to replicate — local trust is earned neighborhood by neighborhood, not deployed from a distribution center.

Due Diligence Note

Beyond NPS and revenue, what metrics track DoorDash's actual impact on local merchant survival rates? If the mission is measurable, the moat is defensible. If it's only a narrative, it isn't.

The Single Question

Does DoorDash's physical world data advantage compound faster than AI reduces the cost of replicating it — and is the answer an infrastructure valuation or a service margin one?

Tony's entire thesis rests on the claim that physical world complexity can't be scraped or replicated quickly. But generative AI is reducing the cost of building software, analyzing data, and mapping physical environments. If the data itself is the moat — not the code that processes it — the advantage extends for another decade. If replication gets cheaper faster than expected, the thesis inverts.

Underneath that sits a valuation question: is DoorDash an infrastructure company (valued on the durability of physical logistics lock-in) or a service business (valued on the variability of delivery margins)? The answer determines not just price — it determines what you're actually buying.

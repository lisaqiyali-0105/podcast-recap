Source: “Atoms Are Cheap, Process Is Pricey” — Max Olson
01
On Cost

Ideas are cheap.
Shipping is expensive.

It turned out that the materials cost of a rocket was around 2% of the typical price. 98 cents of every dollar was going somewhere other than what it was made of — supplier markup stacking through contract layers, custom designs that couldn’t achieve manufacturing scale, and expendable hardware thrown away after every flight.

Max Olson on SpaceX’s first principles analysis

The PM Translation

In product, the equivalent of “atoms” is ideas. They’re essentially free. The expensive part is engineering time, coordination, context-switching, and review cycles. A PM who generates 100 ideas but can’t ruthlessly prioritize is doing exactly what traditional aerospace did: adding cost at every layer with no accountability for where the dollars are going. Ask yourself: what percentage of your team’s time is going to actual product vs. process overhead?

In Practice

Run an “idiot index” on your roadmap. For every feature, ask: what’s the ratio of eng weeks spent to actual user value delivered? High ratio = you’re being an idiot (Elon’s words, not mine). The most over-engineered features in any backlog are usually the ones nobody questioned because a smart stakeholder asked for them.

02
On Requirements

The best feature
is no feature.

“The best part is no part. The best process is no process.”

Elon Musk

“If you are not adding back at least 10% of the requirements you deleted, you aren’t deleting enough.”

Elon Musk

The PM Translation

PMs inherit requirements from executives, customers, legal, and “how we’ve always done it.” SpaceX’s rule: every requirement must have an owner who can defend why it still exists. If the owner can’t explain it, or the original reason no longer applies, delete it. The requirements from “smart people” are the most dangerous: nobody thinks to question them.

In Practice

At your next sprint planning, pick one requirement that’s been on the list for months and ask: who owns this? Can they defend it in 60 seconds? If not, put it in a “delete pending” column for two weeks and see if anyone notices it’s missing.

03
On Validation

Ship to learn.
Not to be right.

You can’t think your way to perfect solutions for problems you don’t fully understand. Your model is always wrong in the ways you don’t know yet. Complex systems have emergent behaviors that only appear when the pieces are actually bolted together. The alternative is to use reality as your primary validation tool.

Max Olson on SpaceX’s engineering philosophy

The PM Translation

The PM equivalent of Boeing’s “analyze before building” is endless PRD writing, stakeholder alignment rounds, and design reviews before a single line of code ships. Every week you spend perfecting a spec is a week you’re not finding out where your model is wrong. SpaceX literally publishes its failures. “How Not to Land an Orbital Rocket,” a compilation video of rockets exploding, set to music. What’s your version of that?

In Practice

For your next feature, identify the single riskiest assumption: the one that, if wrong, makes the whole thing pointless. Now ask: what’s the smallest thing you could ship in two weeks that would tell you whether that assumption is true or false? That’s your Starship prototype.

04
On Cadence

Shipping cadence
is a superpower.

~2–3
days between Falcon 9 launches at peak cadence
20×
same booster reused — each flight a learning loop
5×
more mass to orbit than all other providers combined in 2025

A high production rate solves many ills. Any given technology development is how many iterations do you have, and what’s your time and progress between iterations? So if you have a high production rate, you can have a lot of iterations. You can try a lot of different things.

Elon Musk

The PM Translation

A PM who ships weekly has 52 data points a year. A PM who ships quarterly has 4. Shipping cadence is a learning rate multiplier. The teams that look magically effective after 2 years aren’t smarter. They’ve had more iterations. SpaceX didn’t nail booster landings because they were geniuses. They had 20 attempts at the same problem. Most product teams get 3.

In Practice

Count how many times your team shipped something users could actually interact with in the last 90 days. Now count the number of planning meetings. If the ratio is off, you have a cadence problem, not a quality problem. Ship smaller things more often. Even a one-line copy change is a feedback loop.

05
On Focus

What’s your
Raptor engine?

When Starship development was bottlenecked on Raptor Engine production, that became the company’s focus. Not propellant loading, not heat shields, not launch infrastructure — Raptors. Elon gave it absolute focus, daily updates, memos to the company, resources redirected from elsewhere. Once engine production broke through, attention shifted to the next constraint. The limiter always gets the hammer.

Max Olson on SpaceX’s tip-of-the-spear focus

The PM Translation

PMs are famously bad at this. Spreading a team across 10 initiatives simultaneously is the norm. That’s how you get 10 things at 40% instead of 2 things at 100%. At any given moment there is one constraint that, if removed, unlocks everything downstream. Your job is to identify it, name it out loud, and put disproportionate resources on it until it’s gone. Then find the next one.

In Practice

Ask your team: if we could only make one thing faster or better this quarter, what would it be? The answer is your Raptor engine. Write it down. Put it at the top of every planning doc. Check every other initiative against it: does this help the Raptor problem? If not, it moves to the back of the queue.

06
On Vision

Clear mission makes
prioritization automatic.

“Building cities on other planets isn’t just aspirational branding. It’s a sorting mechanism. The mission attracts missionaries.”

Max Olson

“When the mission is that clear, prioritization becomes automatic. Every decision has a simple test.”

Max Olson

The PM Translation

A product vision is a decision-making tool, not decor. When your vision is sharp enough, 80% of prioritization debates resolve themselves because the answer is obvious against the stated goal. If your team argues about priority in every planning meeting, that’s usually a signal the vision is too vague to be useful. The vision is also a recruiting filter: people who believe in it will work harder than people who just think it’s a reasonable job.

In Practice

Write your product vision in one sentence. Now test it: take two features from your backlog that you’re currently debating and apply the vision as a filter. Does one clearly win? If you still can’t tell, the vision isn’t sharp enough yet. Keep refining until it gives you answers, not more questions.

07
On Systems

Structure beats
heroics. Every time.

The answer to what’s hard to copy isn’t any single tactic. It’s that the tactics are a system. Copy one without the others and it breaks down. The lesson isn’t ‘be like Elon.’ Hero worship is the wrong takeaway. One person didn’t build Starship. The lesson is that structure matters more than the hero. Get the system right, and the results follow.

Max Olson — the essay’s closing argument

The PM Translation

The best product teams aren’t the ones with the most brilliant PM. They’re the ones with the best feedback loops, the clearest constraints, and the cultural tolerance for visible failure. Your job as a PM is to build the system that makes great outcomes repeatable. Not to be the hero who personally solves every hard problem. If your team falls apart when you’re out sick for a week, that’s a system problem, not a vacation problem.

The One Question

Max Olson distills the entire SpaceX playbook to a single question. It’s also the single best question for any PM to ask themselves about their team: How fast are your feedback loops? How fast can you get to reality?

The Single Question

How fast can you
get to reality?

“The common thread is treating reality as the teacher and getting to class as often as possible.”

— Max Olson, “Atoms Are Cheap, Process Is Pricey”

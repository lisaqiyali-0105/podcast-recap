The biggest changes weren’t the model or the prompts. They were: how the task was broken down, how state was tracked, how key steps were validated, and how failures were recovered from. Same model, same prompts — success rate went from under 70% to above 95%.

Harness Engineering

The Manager Translation

When someone on your team keeps underdelivering, the instinct is to coach harder, give more feedback, or eventually replace them. This is the same mistake as blaming the model. The fix in this story wasn’t the person — it was task decomposition, handoff clarity, checkpoints, and recovery paths. Before your next performance conversation, ask: have I designed the system this person is operating in, or am I just hoping they figure it out?

This Week

Pick one underperforming team member. Before your next 1:1, map the work they’re doing: how the task gets handed to them, what information they have, when they’re supposed to check in, and what happens when something goes wrong. Fix one structural gap before adjusting the coaching conversation.

02
On Leadership

Your job is designing
the environment, not
assigning the tasks.

Humans don’t need to write a single line of code. Humans need to design the environment. When something fails, don’t ask it to try harder — ask what capability is missing from the environment.

OpenAI’s engineering philosophy

The Manager Translation

This is the most direct management lesson in the podcast. The environment you design includes: how work is scoped, what context people have, what the feedback loop looks like, what the rules of escalation are, and what “good” is defined as. That’s your job. The team is capable. The harness — your management system — is what’s missing. When your team fails, your first question shouldn’t be “why didn’t they work harder?” — it should be “what did I fail to put in place?”

This Week

After your next project post-mortem, replace the question “what did the team do wrong?” with “what was missing from the environment?” Run through: did they have the right task definition? The right information at the right time? A clear success criterion? A way to recover when things went sideways? Fix the environment before fixing the people.

03
On Communication

More information
doesn’t mean
better work.

“Optimizing context isn’t about giving more — it’s about giving on-demand, in layers, at the right moment.”

Harness Engineering

“More information means scattered attention. The context window is a scarce resource.”

Harness Engineering

The Manager Translation

Most managers over-brief. Long kickoff documents, packed strategy decks, “here’s everything you need to know” emails. The research here suggests this actively hurts performance — more information at once means less useful attention on any of it. The better model: share the core goal upfront, provide reference material only when the person is at the decision point where it’s relevant. Just-in-time context beats just-in-case context.

This Week

Look at the last project brief you sent. Count the number of things in it that the team won’t need until week 3. Move those to a follow-up or a linked doc. Write briefs with one goal, the immediate decision needed, and a pointer to deeper context if required. See if the questions you get back become more focused.

04
On Quality

Self-review is
structurally optimistic.

Separate the person doing the work from the person validating it. The evaluator doesn’t just review code — it actually operates the UI, checks real interactions, verifies actual results. Production and acceptance must be separated. As long as the evaluator is sufficiently independent, the system forms a genuinely effective loop.

Anthropic’s Planner/Generator/Evaluator architecture

The Manager Translation

If you only ask people to self-assess their work, you’ll consistently get optimistic results — especially on qualitative, hard-to-measure work. This isn’t a character flaw; it’s structural. Anthropic’s solution was architectural: a completely separate Evaluator that actually runs the output in the real environment. For a manager, your review mechanisms need independence built in — peer review, external stakeholders, defined rubrics someone other than the author applies.

This Week

For your team’s next major deliverable, define the acceptance criteria before the work starts — and assign someone other than the author to verify them. Even a lightweight rubric evaluated by a teammate beats the author’s own confidence rating every time.

05
On Resilience

Design for failure
before it
designs you.

In real environments, failure is the norm, not the exception. Without recovery mechanisms, every agent error means starting from scratch. A mature harness always includes three things: constraints (what can and can’t be done), checkpoints (how to verify before and after), and recovery (how to restart, reroute, or roll back to a stable state).

Harness Engineering

The Manager Translation

Teams that treat every missed deadline or dropped ball as a crisis burn out fast and learn slowly. Teams that have designed for failure — clear escalation paths, blameless retros, defined rollback options, “if X happens, do Y” protocols — treat the same events as normal operating conditions. The difference isn’t team quality. It’s whether the manager built a recovery harness or just hoped things wouldn’t break.

This Week

Pick one recurring failure mode on your team (missed handoffs, unclear specs, late dependencies). Write a one-paragraph recovery protocol — not a blame assignment, but a defined response: who notices, who decides, what the fallback is. Share it with the team. Normalizing the failure scenario removes the panic and unlocks faster recovery.

06
On Maturity

Management maturity
has three layers.
Most stop at one.

Prompt engineering is engineering the instruction. Context engineering is engineering the input environment. Harness engineering is engineering the entire operating system. Each layer’s boundary is larger than the last — and they’re not substitutes. They stack.

Harness Engineering

The Manager Translation

Early-stage managers fix the instruction — clearer asks, better briefs. Mid-stage managers fix the context — making sure people have the right information, the right stakeholders, the right resources. Senior managers build the system — the norms, the rituals, the feedback loops, the failure protocols that run whether or not the manager is in the room. Most managers plateau at layer 1 or 2. The podcast is an argument for why layer 3 is where sustainable team performance actually lives.

The One Question

The entire playbook distills to a single question for any manager to ask themselves: If your best team member left tomorrow, how much of their performance would survive — because you built the system — versus disappear with them?

The Single Question

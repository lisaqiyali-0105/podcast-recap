01
On Cost

Ideas are cheap.
Shipping is expensive.

It turned out that the materials cost of a rocket was around 2% of the typical price. 98 cents of every dollar was going somewhere other than what it was made of — supplier markup stacking through contract layers, custom designs that couldn't achieve manufacturing scale, and expendable hardware thrown away after every flight.

Max Olson on SpaceX's first principles analysis

The PM Translation

In product, the equivalent of "atoms" is ideas — they're essentially free. The expensive part is engineering time, coordination, context-switching, and review cycles. A PM who generates 100 ideas but can't ruthlessly prioritize is doing exactly what traditional aerospace did: adding cost at every layer with no accountability.

In Practice

Run an "idiot index" on your roadmap. For every feature, ask: what's the ratio of eng weeks spent to actual user value delivered? High ratio = you're being an idiot. The most over-engineered features are usually the ones nobody questioned because a smart stakeholder asked for them.

02
On Requirements

The best feature
is no feature.

"The best part is no part. The best process is no process." If you are not adding back at least 10% of the requirements you deleted, you aren't deleting enough.

Elon Musk

The PM Translation

PMs inherit requirements from executives, customers, legal, and "how we've always done it." SpaceX's rule: every requirement must have an owner who can defend why it still exists. The requirements from "smart people" are the most dangerous — because nobody thinks to question them.

In Practice

At your next sprint planning, pick one requirement that's been on the list for months and ask: who owns this? Can they defend it in 60 seconds? If not, put it in a "delete pending" column for two weeks and see if anyone notices it's missing.
